What Does a Brand Website Need? A Complete Guide for the Restaurant Industry

Travis Chen 2026-04-20 8 min read

You've opened an amazing restaurant — great food, beautiful decor, excellent service. But in 2026, if you don't have a proper brand website, you're missing out on half your exposure opportunities. The first thing consumers do when searching "restaurants nearby" is check your website.

The problem is: many restaurant owners don't know what their website should and shouldn't have. Some spend a fortune on flashy but impractical sites, while others just throw up a Facebook page and call it their official website. 1. Menu Display: The Core Feature

The core of a restaurant website is the menu. But many restaurants make a fatal mistake: putting their menu in a PDF download.

Why are PDF menus a bad idea?

  • Poor mobile experience — PDFs on phones require pinching and scrolling, making for a terrible experience
  • Invisible to search engines — Google can't effectively index dish names and prices within PDFs
  • Difficult to update — Every menu change requires recreating and re-uploading the PDF

The right approach is making the menu native web content with category filters, food photos, and price tags. This provides an excellent mobile browsing experience while letting Google index your dish keywords.

2. Online Reservations: Key to Reducing Labor Costs

An average reservation phone call takes 2-3 minutes. During peak hours, when lines are busy, you're directly losing customers. An online reservation system solves this:

  • 24/7 reservations — Want to book at 2 AM? No problem
  • Automatic confirmations — Email or LINE auto-sends reservation confirmations
  • Reduce no-shows — Automatic reminders the day before reduce the no-show rate
  • Data analytics — Which time slots are most popular? Average party size? Data auto-compiled

Even a simple form-based reservation (name, date, party size, phone) is a hundred times better than relying solely on phone calls.

3. Google Maps Navigation: Don't Let Customers Get Lost

You think listing an address is enough? Customers want one-tap navigation. Embed Google Maps on your website so customers can tap to open their navigation app directly. This small detail dramatically reduces the number of customers who give up because they "can't find the place."

At the same time, a Google Maps embed also helps SEO — Google will more clearly understand where your restaurant is located, improving your local search rankings.

4. Mobile-First: Over 70% of Traffic Comes from Phones

Restaurant websites have an especially high mobile traffic ratio because most people search for restaurants while out, commuting, or on the go. If your website on mobile:

  • Has text too small to read without zooming
  • Has buttons too small to tap
  • Requires horizontal scrolling to see the full page
  • Takes more than 3 seconds to load

You've essentially lost over half your potential customers. Mobile-First design isn't optional — it's mandatory.

5. Brand Atmosphere: The Magic of BGM Background Music

This is a detail most restaurant websites overlook. Appropriate background music lets visitors "feel" your restaurant's atmosphere while browsing the website:

  • Japanese izakaya — Light jazz or shamisen music
  • Italian restaurant — Classical guitar or light opera
  • Cafe — Lo-fi hip hop or soft piano
  • BBQ restaurant — Upbeat rock or tropical music

HEY!BOSS uses AI to generate unique BGM for each restaurant brand. Not stock music, but original background music tailored to your brand identity. Websites include play/pause controls — visitors are never forced to listen.

7. Common Mistakes and Recommendations

Finally, here are the most common mistakes restaurant owners make with their websites:

  1. Using a Facebook page as your official website — Your fan page isn't your asset. When the algorithm changes, you disappear
  2. Not updating the menu — Customers see a dish on the website but it's not available at the restaurant. Trust drops to zero
  3. No SSL certificate — Browser shows "Not Secure," customers leave immediately
  4. Poor photo quality — Food photos are the first impression. Blurry or oddly colored photos drive customers away
  5. No call-to-action (CTA) — Visitors finish browsing but don't know what to do next (reserve? call? get directions?)
